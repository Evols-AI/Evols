# Say No Playbook

## Purpose

Frameworks and language for declining work, pushing back on requests, negotiating scope, and having capacity conversations with your manager. The goal is never to be unhelpful -- it's to protect time for higher-leverage work.

---

## Decision Tree: Should I Say Yes?

```
New request comes in
    │
    ├── Is it 🔴 Critical Today? → YES, do it
    ├── Is it 🟡 High Leverage? → YES, do it
    ├── Does it serve the Three Things That Matter this week? → YES, do it
    │
    ├── Can someone else do it? → REDIRECT
    ├── Can it wait? → DEFER (⚪ or 🟣)
    ├── Can I do a reduced version? → NEGOTIATE SCOPE
    │
    └── None of the above → SAY NO or escalate capacity
```

---

## The Four Response Patterns

### 1. Redirect (Someone Else Can Do This)

**When:** The request is valid but doesn't require your specific expertise or authority.

**Template:**
> "Thanks for bringing this to me. I think [Person] is actually better positioned for this because [reason]. Let me connect you -- or if you'd prefer, I can loop them in."

**Stronger version (when you keep getting pulled in):**
> "I want to make sure this gets the right attention. My plate is full with [🔴/🟡 items], and [Person] has more context on this area. I'd recommend going to them directly."

### 2. Defer (It Can Wait)

**When:** The request is real but not time-sensitive, and you're at capacity.

**Template:**
> "I can definitely help with this, but not this week. I'm focused on [Three Things That Matter]. Can we revisit this [specific timeframe]? I'll add it to my backlog so it doesn't fall through the cracks."

**For meeting requests:**
> "This week is packed. Can we push this to [next available slot]? I want to give it proper attention rather than squeezing it in."

### 3. Negotiate Scope (Reduced Version)

**When:** You need to be involved, but the full ask is more than what's needed.

**Template:**
> "I can do [reduced version] by [date]. Would that work? The full version would take [longer timeline] given what I've got going on. Let me know which you'd prefer."

**For document requests:**
> "Rather than a full [doc type], what if I put together a [lighter artifact -- one-pager, bullet summary, Slack post]? That would cover the key decisions and I can turn it around faster."

### 4. Say No (This Shouldn't Exist)

**When:** The work is low-leverage, duplicative, or below your level.

**Template:**
> "I appreciate you thinking of me, but I'm going to pass on this one. I need to stay focused on [high-leverage work]. If this is blocking something critical, let's talk about who else could pick it up."

**For recurring meetings that aren't valuable:**
> "I've been thinking about my time in [meeting]. I don't think I'm adding enough value to justify the slot, and I need the time back for [specific work]. Would it work if I dropped off and you pulled me in async when you need my input?"

## The Capacity Conversation

### When to Have It

- Task board shows persistent overload (🔴 items regularly exceed what one person can handle)
- You're consistently working outside your stated boundaries
- Quality of 🟡 work is suffering because 🔴 and ⚪ consume all available time
- You've been deferring 🟡 work for 2+ weeks

### Framework: The Data-Driven Approach

**Step 1: Lead with the task board data**
> "I want to walk you through my current workload. Here's what I'm tracking right now: [number] active items, [number] of which are critical-path. I'm spending about [X]% of my time on reactive work and [Y]% on the strategic work that should be my primary focus."

**Step 2: Name the tradeoff**
> "At current capacity, I can do [A] or [B] well, but not both. Here's what I'd recommend we prioritize, and here's what I think we should defer, delegate, or drop."

**Step 3: Propose a solution (not just a problem)**
> "What would help most is [specific ask: reduced scope on X, delegating Y to another PM, pushing back the deadline on Z, temporary help for a specific deliverable]."

**Step 4: Frame it as organizational intelligence, not personal complaint**
> "I want to make sure the work that matters most gets the quality it deserves. Right now I'm spread thin enough that I'm worried about [specific risk]."

### What NOT to Do

- Don't frame it as "I can't handle this" -- frame it as "Here's the tradeoff the team is making"
- Don't wait until you're burned out -- raise it when you see the trend
- Don't just list problems -- always bring a recommendation
- Don't apologize for having a workload conversation -- this is a senior-level skill

## Situational Playbook

### "Can You Just..."
The most dangerous phrase. Small asks that fragment deep work.

> "I could, but I'm in the middle of [🟡 work] and context-switching would cost me the afternoon. Can I batch this into my end-of-day sweep? If it's truly urgent, let me know what's at stake and I'll reprioritize."

### Skip-Level or Executive Ask
Higher stakes. Can't just say no.

> "Absolutely -- let me understand what you need and by when. I have [current commitments] this week, so I want to make sure I'm giving this the right priority relative to [their other priorities]."

Then assess: Is this actually 🔴/🟡, or does it just feel urgent because of who asked?

### "We Need You in This Meeting"
Often a sign you're being used as insurance, not because you're needed.

> "Happy to help. Can you share the agenda so I can confirm I'm the right person? If it's primarily about [topic], [Other Person] might be better positioned. If you need me specifically, I can make [specific time] work."

### Peer Asks That Should Go Through Your Manager

> "I want to help, but this is a significant time commitment and I'd want to align with [manager] on prioritization. Can you bring it up in [appropriate forum] so we can slot it properly?"

## The 24-Hour Rule

When a new request triggers an immediate "yes" impulse:

1. **Pause.** Say "Let me check my priorities and get back to you by [tomorrow / end of day]."
2. **Check the task board.** Where does this actually land in the tiers?
3. **Apply the decision tree.** Is this yours to do, or can it be redirected?
4. **Respond deliberately.** Not from guilt or obligation, but from a clear assessment of leverage.

Exception: Genuine 🔴 items with hard deadlines. Those get immediate action.
